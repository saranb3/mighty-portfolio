<CaseStudySection
  id="research"
  label="Research"
  headline="One interview reframed the entire brief — from 'safety app' to 'single trusted hub.'"
>

The interview that changed the product was with a Chinese tourist who'd been in Bangkok for four days. When I asked how he found out about a recent incident near his hotel, he listed five different sources — Line groups, WeChat news, his embassy's website, a Thai news site translated through Google, and a friend back home. None of them agreed on what had happened or what to do.

> "In China I just open one app. Here I open ten and trust none of them."

That reframed the brief. The problem wasn't a missing safety app — it was that **safety information existed but was scattered across channels with no synthesis**. The Chinese super-app model (one app, many services, trusted because it's official) was the mental model our users already had. The opportunity was to bring that model to inbound tourism.

This became our north star: **TripBuddy as a single trusted hub** — emergency, news, embassy, etiquette, basic services — rather than a thin safety layer on top of existing fragmented tools.

</CaseStudySection>

<CaseStudySection
  id="design"
  label="Design"
  headline="Designing the News / Area Detail screen for a panicking foreigner."
>

The screen I'm proudest of is the **News / Area Detail screen** — what a user sees when they tap into a flagged area on the map.

<div className="my-8 rounded-2xl border border-line bg-paper-soft aspect-[16/10] flex items-center justify-center font-mono text-xs tracking-widest text-ink-soft uppercase">
  Screenshot: News / Area Detail screen
</div>

The design constraint: a foreign tourist, possibly under stress, possibly with limited English, needs to answer three questions in under five seconds.

1. How bad is this?
2. What's actually happening?
3. What should I do right now?

I structured the screen as a strict top-to-bottom answer to those three questions:

- **Location + risk gauge** at the top — instant severity read without reading any text
- **High-risk banner** in plain language — one sentence, one action verb
- **Current Situation** as a dated bullet list — facts, not narrative
- **Recommended Actions** as a separate block — verbs first ("Stay indoors," "Call 191 only with new info")
- **Source updates** at the bottom — for users who want to verify before acting

Separating *situation* from *action* was the key call. Most news apps blend them, which forces a stressed user to extract instructions from prose. We made the instructions a discrete block they could act on without re-reading.

</CaseStudySection>

<CaseStudySection
  id="reflection"
  label="Reflection"
  headline="Three things I'd do differently next time."
>

1. **The illustrations on Home and SOS are wrong for the brand promise.** Cute 3D icons on a "Crime" or "Injury" tile undercut the trust we were trying to build. NanNam and I discussed this late and didn't have time to swap them. A second pass would replace them with flatter, more neutral iconography.
2. **The Area Safety map needs a confidence indicator.** Our zones were AI-generated from news scraping. Users have to know how recent and how reliable each zone's data is. Without that, the map is asking for blind trust we hadn't earned.
3. **I should have wireframed before jumping into hi-fi.** When Boom handed off the Figma file, I went straight to polished screens because the deadline was tight. The result was that some interaction decisions (like the SOS-button-plus-six-categories pattern) didn't get the scrutiny they deserved. Lo-fi first would have surfaced those questions earlier.

</CaseStudySection>

- **Won "Most Popular" at the BBL Student Internship Program hackathon**

The bank's feasibility note was the right pushback. If I were to scope a v2, I'd cut the scope to a single high-confidence loop — verified news + one-tap embassy contact — and ship that before earning the right to expand.
